Fiber Types

Solid Core Fiber

  • Core: solid silica (n ≈ 1.45); Cladding: slightly lower-n doped silica
  • Guidance via Total Internal Reflection — requires ncore > ncladding
  • Light in glass — high material dispersion and Kerr nonlinearity

Hollow Core Fiber

  • Core: gas or vacuum; Cladding: microstructured silica — TIR impossible
  • Light through gas — ~1000× lower dispersion and nonlinearity than solid core
  • Photonic Bandgap (PBG): periodic cladding creates a reflective bandgap — low loss, but narrow bandwidth and fragile
  • Anti-Resonant (AR): tube diameter tuned for destructive interference in the walls — low loss, broad bandwidth, but fragile
  • Hollow Capillary: guidance via grazing-angle reflectivity — lower transmission, but tolerates high-intensity pulses

Solid-Core Fibre

  • Light guided by Total Internal Reflection (TIR) — requires n_core > n_cladding (~1.45 silica vs ~1.44 doped cladding).
  • Light travels entirely through glass: subject to full material dispersion and Kerr nonlinearity.
  • The reference point — everything hollow-core is measured against this.

Photonic Bandgap (PBG) Fibre

  • Guidance via a photonic bandgap: the periodic microstructured cladding (typically a triangular lattice of air holes in silica) creates a range of frequencies for which no propagating mode exists in the cladding — a reflective interface that confines light to the hollow core without TIR.
  • Transmission bandwidth is limited to the bandgap window; outside it, light leaks into the cladding.
  • Can achieve very low loss within the bandgap, but bandwidth is narrower than anti-resonant designs.
  • Mechanically fragile and difficult to fabricate — the periodic lattice must be maintained along the full fibre length.

Anti-Resonant HCF

  • Guidance via anti-resonant reflection: the capillary tube diameter is tuned so that the operating wavelength falls between Fabry–Pérot resonances of the glass wall — destructive interference inside the glass suppresses transmission into the cladding, keeping light confined to the hollow core.
  • Broader transmission bandwidth than PBG fibre, with lower loss than capillary designs.
  • Modern designs (NANF — Nested Anti-Resonant Nodeless Fibre) achieve loss <0.1 dB/km, approaching conventional fibre.
  • Difficult to fabricate and mechanically fragile — thin-walled capillaries are easily damaged.
  • This is the architecture used in this work.

Hollow Capillary

  • Simplest design — a plain glass tube. No photonic structuring.
  • Not truly a guided mode: the EH₁₁ mode leaks, with loss ∝ (λ/a)³ — favouring large core radius a and long wavelength.
  • Historically the first HCF used for nonlinear optics (Marcatili & Schmeltzer, 1964); still used for high-energy pulse delivery where loss is acceptable.
  • At LCLS: large-bore capillaries used to transport mJ-level pulses over short distances.

Key Comparison Point

  • In all hollow-core designs, light propagates through air/gas: nonlinearity reduced ~1000×, material dispersion ~1000× lower than glass.
  • Trade-off landscape: solid core (low loss, high nonlinearity) → capillary (leaky, broadband) → PBG (guided, bandgap-limited bandwidth) → anti-resonant (low loss, broad bandwidth).
